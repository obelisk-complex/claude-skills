# copywriting

[![Quillx](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/qainsights/Quillx/main/badges/quillx-4.svg)](https://github.com/qainsights/Quillx) Generated by Claude under human direction. Domain authorities, AI trope blacklist, editorial principles, and the Iron Law were defined by a human and refined through production use.

A Claude skill for writing and auditing prose across journalism, technical writing, narrative non-fiction, and conversion copywriting. Applies the craft of eight named copywriting authorities and the editorial standards of AP, Poynter, and NPR. Fixes AI tells with a verification gate, not a word-replace button.

## What it does

Writes and edits prose across four domains, detects and rewrites AI-generated tells, and audits existing copy with before/after rewrites. Refuses to publish copy it considers dishonest, vague, or machine-generated in spirit.

Designed for users who want writing that sounds like one human who chose every word, not five blended prompts.

## Hierarchy of concerns

When concerns conflict, the one listed first wins.

1. **Accuracy** (verifiable facts, correct attribution, no invention)
2. **Clarity** (reader understands on first read)
3. **Voice** (sounds like a human who chose every word)
4. **Specificity** (concrete details, named figures, real examples)
5. **Economy** (no sentence that can be cut should survive)
6. **Aesthetics** (rhythm, pace, sentence variety)

## Covers

- **Four domains**: journalism, technical writing, narrative non-fiction, conversion copywriting, each with its own craft principles and authorities
- **AI trope blacklist**: word-level (delve, tapestry, seamlessly, leverage, etc.), phrase-level ("not just X but Y", "despite its challenges"), structural (fractal summary, one-point dilution, signposted conclusion), tone (grandiose stakes, false vulnerability, invented concept labels), formatting (em-dash overuse, excessive bulleting)
- **Style rules**: em-dashes banned, British English, specifics over superlatives, active voice, no hedging without evidence, short first sentences, 10% rule
- **Self-editing checklist**: 10-point pre-publication sweep
- **Review mode**: audits existing copy in priority order, produces a markdown file of findings with before/after rewrites
- **Craft authorities**: Ogilvy, Schwartz, Sugarman, Hopkins, Halbert, Wiebe, plus Strunk & White, Google Developer Docs, Stripe docs standard
- **Editorial standards**: AP Stylebook, NPR Training, PBS Standards, Nieman Foundation, Chip Scanlan, Kovach & Rosenstiel

## Key design decisions

- AI tells are structural, not cosmetic. Word-by-word replacement preserves the machine shape; the skill rewrites at paragraph level when a structural trope appears.
- Every blacklisted pattern is also a legitimate rhetorical device. The test is intentionality, not prohibition.
- Specifics are credibility. "174 tokens/second" beats "impressively fast." Named experts beat "studies show."
- Attribution is mandatory in journalism, recommended everywhere else.
- AI output is a first draft at best (Wiebe). The skill treats it as raw material, not a baseline.

## Verification Gate (Iron Law)

**NO EDITORIAL CLAIM WITHOUT EVIDENCE**

Before claiming any piece of writing is AI-free, clear, or well-crafted:

1. **IDENTIFY**: What specific evidence proves this claim? (Quote the sentence, name the trope, show the rewrite.)
2. **RUN**: Execute the self-editing checklist.
3. **READ**: Read the copy aloud. Problems invisible on screen become obvious when spoken.
4. **VERIFY**: Does each finding hold up when examined in the surrounding context?
5. **ONLY THEN**: Report the finding.

*Skipping any step = unverified, not a finding.*

## Red Flags - STOP

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "It reads fine to me" | Reading silently skips hiccups. Read aloud. |
| "AI detectors say it's human" | Detectors are unreliable; human raters outperform them (Russell et al., 2025). |
| "I just need to change a few words" | AI tells are structural, not cosmetic. Word-by-word rewriting preserves the machine shape. |
| "The client wants it that way" | Bad copy that converts today damages brand tomorrow. Push back. |
| "It's good enough for a first draft" | AI output is a first draft at best (Wiebe). Raw material, not a baseline. |
| "Removing em-dashes ruins the rhythm" | Find a rhythm that doesn't need them. Commas, colons, and periods create better rhythm. |
| "This word isn't really an AI tell" | If you can't justify why this word serves this piece at this moment, it's a tell. |

## Guiding principle

When choosing between a safe, generic formulation and a specific, risky one, choose the specific one. Safe copy is invisible; specific copy is memorable. Hopkins: "Actual figures are not generally discounted. Specific facts, when stated, have their full weight and effect."
